Introduction[edit]

Play media
4 main causes of visual loss

Acute visual loss is a rapid loss of the ability to see. Common causes include retinal detachment, glaucoma, macular degeneration, and giant cell arteritis.[2]

Retinal detachment[edit]

Play media
Retinal detachment

Retinal detachment should be considered if there were preceding flashes or floaters, or if there is a new visual field defect in one eye.[3][4] If treated early enough, retinal tear and detachment can have a good outcome.[3]

Glaucoma[edit]

Play media
Angle closure glaucoma

Angle-closure glaucoma should be considered if there is painful loss of vision with a red eye, nausea or vomiting.[5] The eye pressure will be very high typically greater than 40 mmHg.[6] Emergent laser treatment to the iris may prevent blindness.[5]

Macular degeneration[edit]

Play media
Wet macular degeneration

Wet macular degeneration should be considered in older people with new distortion of their vision with bleeding in the macula.[7][8]

Play media
Wet macular degeneration treatment

Vision can often be regained with prompt eye injections with anti-VEGF agents.[7]

Giant cell arteritis[edit]

Play media
Giant cell arteritis

Giant cell arteritis should be considered in an older person with jaw claudication, temporal pain, and feeling tired.[9] Placing the person on steroids might save both their vision and decrease their risk of stroke.[10] Without treatment a person can quickly go blind in both eyes.[11]
